## Why SIFT exists

Developed by Mike Caulfield to counter "vertical reading" — the instinct to evaluate a source's credibility by examining the source itself (design quality, logos, "About" pages, writing style). Research from the Stanford History Education Group demonstrated that this approach fails systematically. Even domain experts are misled by professional-looking websites that pass surface-level credibility checks.

SIFT replaces vertical reading with "lateral reading": the practice of opening new tabs to investigate the source before engaging with its content. The verification happens outside the document, not inside it.

## S — Stop

**Goal:** Interrupt emotional engagement before it overrides critical thinking.

**What to do:**
1. Before reading further, identify your emotional reaction. Are you angry? Vindicated? Afraid? Amused?
2. Content designed to provoke strong emotion often bypasses deliberative processing. The emotional response IS the manipulation.
3. Flag the content with your observed reaction. This does not mean it is false — but it means you proceed with explicit awareness of the framing.

**Signs of emotionally manipulative content:**
- Headlines with "shocking," "devastating," "you won't believe," "the truth about"
- All-caps or excessive exclamation marks
- Images chosen for visceral impact rather than information
- Binary framing: "The media won't tell you this," "What they don't want you to know"
- Outrage bait designed to trigger sharing before thinking

## I — Investigate the source

**Goal:** Assess the creator's expertise, track record, and agenda before trusting their content.

**What to do:**
1. Open a new tab. Search for the author or organization name.
2. What do OTHER sources say about them? Wikipedia entries, news profiles, and industry databases provide independent assessments.
3. Check for: professional background, relevant qualifications, prior corrections or controversies, funding sources, and organizational affiliations.
4. Do not rely on the source's own "About" page. A biased or fraudulent source will present itself as credible on its own site.

**Key questions:**
- Is this person or organization known for accurate reporting on this topic?
- Do they have domain expertise, or are they a generalist speaking as an authority?
- Who funds them? Does that funding create a conflict of interest for this specific claim?
- Have they issued corrections? How do they handle errors?

**Red flags:**
- No author listed or a pseudonym with no track record
- Site that aggregates content without original reporting
- "About" page with vague credentials ("industry expert," "researcher")
- Organization that does not disclose funding sources

## F — Find better coverage

**Goal:** Determine if the claim is independently corroborated.

**What to do:**
1. Search for the core claim using a neutral phrasing. Do not use the same language as the original — biased framing can reproduce the same bad results.
2. Look for consensus. Do multiple reputable, unrelated outlets report the same facts?
3. Apply "click restraint": scan search results before clicking. Look at URLs (is it a known outlet?), snippets (does it address the claim directly?), and dates (is this current?).
4. If a claim is significant, mainstream outlets with editorial standards should have covered it. If only fringe sites or copycat aggregators report it, the claim is isolated.

**What "better coverage" means:**
- Outlets with published corrections policies
- Multiple independent sources — not the same wire story reprinted
- Sources with domain expertise (science claim → science publication, not a general news blog)
- Original reporting, not aggregation or rewrites

## T — Trace claims to original context

**Goal:** Find the primary source and verify that re-reporting has not distorted it.

**What to do:**
1. For statistics: trace to the underlying study, report, or dataset. Read the methodology section, not just the abstract.
2. For quotes: find the full transcript, recording, or original interview. Check if the quote preserves the speaker's meaning. A partial quote can invert intent.
3. For images and video: reverse image search to find the earliest known instance. Check if the media has been edited, cropped, or repurposed for a different context.
4. For referenced documents: find the original PDF or official release. Do not rely on summaries or excerpts provided by third parties.

**Chain of custody degradation:**
```
Original study → University press release → Science news site → General news site → Blog → Social media post
```

Each step introduces potential distortion. The fact-checker's job is to move up the chain, not down it. Always cite the highest step you can access.

## Lateral reading vs. vertical reading

**Vertical reading** (what most people do):
- Stay on the page
- Evaluate based on internal cues: design, logos, writing quality, "About" page
- Trust if the site "looks professional"
- **Result: Easily fooled by well-made disinformation.**

**Lateral reading** (what professionals do):
- Immediately open new tabs
- Search for information ABOUT the source, not FROM the source
- Let the web's collective assessment inform your judgment
- **Result: Source credibility is established independently before content is engaged.**

The distinction is not subtle. SHEG research found that professional fact-checkers, given 60 seconds with an unfamiliar website, consistently identified its credibility correctly. PhD historians given unlimited time on the same task were frequently misled — because they read vertically. The difference is not intelligence or education; it is method.
