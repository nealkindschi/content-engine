## Tiered source hierarchy

### Primary sources
Original materials or firsthand accounts. These are the most reliable because no intermediary filters or interprets them — but they still require corroboration.

**Examples:**
- Court records, legal filings, sworn testimony
- Raw study data, clinical trial registrations (ClinicalTrials.gov)
- Government datasets (BLS, Census, Eurostat, FRED)
- Direct interview recordings or transcripts (with timestamps)
- Official company filings (SEC 10-K, annual reports)
- Patents, technical specifications
- Eyewitness accounts (note: eyewitness memory is fallible — corroborate)

**Red flags even in primary sources:**
- Self-reported data (surveys, company-issued "studies")
- Press releases (these are primary sources for the organization's claims, not for the truth of those claims)
- Leaked documents (verify authenticity; consider the leaker's agenda)

### Secondary sources
Analysis, interpretation, or reporting on primary sources by outlets with institutional editorial standards.

**Examples:**
- Peer-reviewed journal articles (note: peer review ensures methodology, not correctness)
- News reports from wire services (Reuters, AP, AFP) or major outlets with corrections policies
- Government analysis reports (GAO, CBO, Congressional Research Service)
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Books from academic presses

**Red flags:**
- Outlets with no published corrections policy
- "Studies show" without a link or DOI
- Reporting that uses only a study's press release, not the actual paper

### Tertiary sources
Compilations, summaries, or reference works. Too far removed from original data to serve as authoritative proof. Use only for background orientation.

**Examples:**
- Wikipedia and other wikis
- General encyclopedias (Britannica, etc.)
- Aggregator sites, "best of" lists
- AI-generated summaries

**Acceptable use:** Find the cited references in a Wikipedia article and verify those directly. Never cite Wikipedia as an authority for a specific factual claim.

## CREDIBLE framework

Evaluate every source using these eight dimensions. A source does not need to score perfectly on all — the assessment should match the claim's stakes.

### Credibility
Does the source have a reputation for accuracy? Has it issued corrections when wrong?

**Check:** Search for `[source name] corrections` and `[source name] retraction`.

### Reliability
Is the information consistent across other independent sources? Has it changed over time?

**Check:** Cross-reference the claim with 2-3 unrelated sources. Check Wayback Machine for changes to the content.

### Evidence
Is supporting evidence cited, linked, or otherwise accessible? Can you follow the trail?

**Red flag:** "According to sources," "experts say," or other attribution that cannot be checked. Quality sources name their evidence.

### Date
Is the information current? Has newer research or events superseded it?

**Check:** Search for more recent publications on the same topic. A 2018 study on COVID-19 is pre-pandemic and likely irrelevant.

### Intent
Is the purpose to inform, persuade, sell, or provoke?

**Check:** Funding sources, organizational mission, ad-to-content ratio, emotional language.

### Bias
Does the source have a known ideological, financial, or political leaning?

**Note:** Bias does not automatically invalidate a source. A think tank with a known perspective can publish accurate data. The question is whether the methodology is transparent and the data is available for scrutiny.

### Logic
Does the argument follow from the evidence, or are there gaps and leaps? Are alternative explanations considered?

**Check:** If claim = "X causes Y," does the evidence actually demonstrate causation or just correlation? Are confounders addressed?

### Expertise
Does the author or organization have domain qualifications? Is this person speaking within their field?

**Red flag:** A physicist making claims about epidemiology. A celebrity endorsing a medical treatment. Credentials in one field do not transfer to another.

## Edge cases and ambiguous sources

### Anonymous sources
A single anonymous source is insufficient verification. Multiple independent anonymous sources describing the same facts from different angles can be considered corroborated. Note that "anonymous" to the reader is different from "known to the journalist" — but the reader can't verify.

### Preprint servers (arXiv, SSRN, medRxiv)
Preprints have NOT been peer-reviewed. They are primary sources in the sense that the data is raw, but their conclusions are provisional. Flag any preprint as "not yet peer-reviewed" and look for the published version.

### Social media posts
Treat as a claim to verify, not as a source. A tweet from an official account is a statement of position, not evidence of truth. Screenshots can be fabricated — verify by finding the original post.

### Think tanks and advocacy groups
Classify by transparency: Do they disclose funders? Is their methodology published? Can their data be independently checked? A transparent advocacy group with published methodology ranks higher than an opaque one.

### AI-generated content
Current AI systems are not fact-checking engines. They can fabricate plausible-sounding citations, DOIs, and statistics. Any AI-generated content must be independently verified against primary sources. Never cite an AI system as a source.

## Quick-reference scoring

Use this shorthand when cataloging multiple sources in a report:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **High** | Established institutional source with verifiable evidence trail |
| **Medium** | Credible but with limitations (single source, moderate bias, secondary) |
| **Low** | Significant concerns (anonymous, biased without transparency, tertiary) |
| **Unusable** | Fabricated, retracted, or entirely unverifiable |
